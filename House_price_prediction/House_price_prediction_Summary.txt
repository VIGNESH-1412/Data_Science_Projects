HOUSEWORTH AI — PROJECT SUMMARY
================================

WHAT IT IS
----------
HouseWorth AI is a house price prediction project. You feed it details about
a house (bedrooms, bathrooms, size, grade, location, etc.) and it predicts
what the house should sell for. It's built on a real King County (Seattle)
housing dataset and shipped as a working Streamlit web app.

THE DATA
--------
- 21,613 house sale records, 21 columns
- No missing values, no duplicates after cleanup
- Dropped id and date columns since they don't help predict price
- About 5.3% of rows (1,146) were price outliers — mostly genuine high-end
  homes, not bad data, so they were kept rather than deleted

WHAT WAS EXPLORED
------------------
- Correlation with price: sqft_living, grade, sqft_above, and sqft_living15
  came out as the strongest linked to price
- Visualized price distribution, price vs living area, price vs grade,
  price vs year built
- Built a correlation heatmap across all numeric features

NEW FEATURES CREATED
---------------------
1. FamilyFriendlyHome — flags homes with 3+ bedrooms, 2+ bathrooms, and
   1800+ sqft living space
2. ModernHome — flags homes built in 2000 or later, or renovated
3. SpaciousHome — a 0/1/2 scale based on how big the living space is

THE MODELS
----------
Two models were trained and compared:

  Random Forest (1500 trees)
    Train R²: 0.983   Test R²: 0.858
    MAE: ~72,343   RMSE: ~146,258

  XGBoost (300 trees, learning rate 0.05, depth 6)
    Train R²: 0.964   Test R²: 0.883
    MAE: ~68,787   RMSE: ~132,891

XGBoost was the better model. It scored higher on test data AND had a
smaller gap between its train and test scores, meaning it's less
overfit and more reliable on new houses it hasn't seen. This is the
model that got saved and used in the app.

Being honest here: Random Forest's near-perfect train score (0.983) next
to its noticeably lower test score (0.858) is a sign of overfitting.
XGBoost isn't perfect either, but it's the more trustworthy of the two.

WHAT ACTUALLY DRIVES PRICE
----------------------------
According to the XGBoost model, in order of importance:
  1. Grade (build/design quality) — 39.5%, by far the biggest factor
  2. Living area (sqft_living) — 16%
  3. Waterfront — 13%
  4. Latitude (location) — 7%
  5. View, longitude, year built, sqft_living15, bathrooms — smaller effects

Interesting takeaway: bedroom count and lot size barely matter. Quality of
build and actual usable space matter far more than how many rooms a house
has on paper.

THE APP
-------
A Streamlit app (app.py) with a custom-styled landing page (light,
minimalist "blueprint" theme, Space Grotesk + JetBrains Mono fonts) built
in HTML and embedded through streamlit.components.v1. The landing page's
buttons use query parameters to route into the actual prediction form,
which takes all the house features and runs them through the saved
XGBoost model to return a price estimate.

HONEST GAPS
------------
- No scaler was saved alongside the model. Not a problem for XGBoost, but
  would matter if the model ever gets swapped for something like linear
  regression.
- The app's expected feature list includes a few engineered columns
  (house_age, is_renovated, total_rooms, total_sqft) that need to be
  added as a feature-engineering step before training, to keep the app
  and the model in sync.
- Outliers were studied but not removed, so a handful of very expensive
  homes still influence the model's predictions.
- Test R² of 0.88 is decent, not outstanding — this kind of dataset
  usually needs geography-aware features (distance to city center,
  nearby schools, etc.) to get meaningfully more accurate.

TECH USED
---------
Python, Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, Seaborn, Scikit-learn (Random Forest),
XGBoost, Streamlit, Pickle
